# light

## chapter one: arrival

They arrived early. Part of it was habit, but mostly they just didn't know what to expect. They weren't sure how crowded it would get or how far they'd have to push the wheelchair from the parking lot to the gathering.

The field was open and mostly quiet. A few people were already there, spreading blankets or folding out small chairs. Some stood around talking in low voices; others just looked for a place to sit. There was a sense of quiet anticipation.

The caregiver guided the wheelchair over the bumps in the ground and found a spot near a patch of shade. They set the brake and adjusted the footrests. They tucked the blanket around the patient's legs and made sure the water bottle was within reach. Small things they had repeated many times.

More people arrived in twos and threes. A couple walked in carrying a picnic bag and a fussing baby. A teenager helped an older man settle into his chair. A few people moved forward, hoping to find a spot near the front. There was a general sense of waiting, but no one seemed tense or urgent. Just people finding their places.

The caregiver looked around without any particular focus—noticing the shoes people wore, the way people sat, how they held their bodies. They wondered which of them were sick.

At some point, the healer stepped onto the platform. He didn't make an entrance; he just walked up and stood there. A small hand gesture; he seemed aware of everyone in the audience. His face was relaxed, his eyes kind. A few people noticed and sat a little straighter, but the shift was subtle.

He began speaking. His voice wasn't loud, just steady enough to reach across the space.

The caregiver listened, but their attention drifted between the voice and the people nearby, and then to the patient beside them. Someone in the crowd wiped their eyes. Someone else leaned forward with their hands clasped loosely. A child played with the corner of a blanket.

Nothing unusual was happening—but it felt unusual.

The caregiver returned their focus to the speaker.

## chapter two: the gathering

The healer spoke in a calm, even voice. The words carried across the field, but not every sentence reached clearly. The caregiver caught bits and pieces—references to divine help, to the body's innate intelligence to heal, to things unseen moving through the day. The tone was steady, gentle yet firm.

There was a restlessness in the crowd, a shifting of weight, a soft sigh now and then, like people weren't sure whether to relax or engage. The healer said something else, something about coming to the light.

Every so often he paused and turned toward a specific person. He didn't call them out; he just spoke as if he already knew their story. The caregiver couldn't hear those quieter exchanges, but they noticed how people responded. Someone pressed a hand to their mouth. Another put their head down and wiped their eyes. A man near the front leaned forward sharply and then sat back again as if steadying himself.

From a distance, small movements caught the caregiver's eye—someone lifting their hands slightly, a chair scraping at an odd moment. It was hard to tell if anything unusual was happening or if people were simply adjusting themselves. There was an occasional bustle of activity, but mostly out of sight.

The caregiver tried to follow the healer's rhythm but found their attention drifting. They listened, but also kept track of the patient beside them—how they were sitting, whether they looked comfortable, whether the blanket slipped. They adjusted the armrest quietly. The patient remained still, though their breathing had deepened in a way the caregiver wasn't sure how to interpret.

Was that real?

The breeze shifted, carrying the sound of the healer's voice in waves—clear, then faint, then clear again. Some people bowed their heads. One woman looked upward as if expecting something to arrive from above, though the sky was open and ordinary.

The healer walked a few steps across the platform, looking out again. He paused at one edge and raised his hand slightly—not as a gesture to the whole group, but as though addressing someone the caregiver couldn't see.

People murmured, then fell still.

The caregiver didn't feel anything in particular—no rush of emotion or sense of meaning. Just the awareness that small things were happening around them, some visible, some not, and that the crowd was taking them seriously.

They kept their hand resting lightly on the wheelchair handle, steady and ready, without knowing why.

## chapter three: the first movements

The healer kept speaking, his voice steady across the field. People were mostly still now, listening or watching or simply waiting. The air had settled into a kind of warm quiet.

The caregiver glanced at the patient and saw a small shift—a slight adjustment of posture. They leaned forward just a little, as if trying to get comfortable. The caregiver reached over to straighten the blanket, thinking it was nothing more than that.

A few moments passed. The healer paused and addressed someone closer to the front. People reacted—soft sniffles, a head bowed, a hand lifted briefly—and then fell quiet again.

The patient moved again. Not a fidget this time. More deliberate. Their shoulders lifted as they took a deeper breath, and their hands slid along the armrests as if testing the feel of them.

The caregiver watched, unsure. They had seen the patient try to shift before, but something about this was different—less effort, more intention. Still, they said nothing.

Someone a few rows ahead turned slightly, looking back as if they'd noticed the movement too. Another person paused mid-gesture, eyes drifting toward them for a second before returning to the healer.

The patient lifted one foot, only an inch, then set it back down. Their hands tightened gently on the armrests. They leaned forward a little more, gathering themselves.

The caregiver's breath caught—they weren't sure why—and they found themselves straightening where they sat, hand slightly tightening on the wheelchair.

The patient lifted their head and looked out toward the platform, their eyes clear and focused. Then they placed both feet firmly on the ground.

For a moment everything seemed to settle around that simple position. The crowd, the healer's voice, the breeze—all of it continued, but there was a focus and the focus was right here.

The patient leaned forward again, steady, intentional.

The caregiver didn't touch them. They just watched, without knowing they were waiting.

## chapter four: standing

The patient stood up. Slowly. Carefully.

For a moment they wobbled, knees uncertain, but then they steadied themselves. Their hands floated slightly away from their sides, both testing their balance and feeling the air.

People nearby shifted and began looking over. A few stood, not rushing, just moving closer out of quiet concern or curiosity. Others watched from where they were, alert but not approaching.

The caregiver didn't move at first. They stayed frozen beside the wheelchair, unsure of what they were seeing. Then, almost on instinct, they rose to their feet.

The patient blinked several times, looking around the field. They touched their legs, their arms, their face—checking, it seemed, that everything was real. Their breath came a little fast but steady. They took a few steps forward, slightly unsteady but capable, then turned and came back toward the caregiver.

The caregiver reached out and guided them gently to sit on the ground. Out of habit, they reached for the water bottle and then the blanket, but hesitated, unsure if either were needed now. They sat beside the patient, not speaking, caught between a profound sense of awe and disbelief.

Around them, the crowd settled into a held silence. People watched with soft, questioning expressions, but no one spoke loudly.

Then, from somewhere off to the side, a voice whispered—not excited, not dramatic, just curious:

"Were they just healed?"

## chapter five: leaving the field

The healer's voice continued for a while, steady and unbroken, as if the moment that had just happened was simply part of the day. People listened with a different kind of stillness now. No one whispered anymore. Even the children seemed held in place by the calm that had settled over the field.

Eventually the healer lowered his hand and brought the gathering to a close. A soft rustling moved through the crowd as people stood, stretched, or bent to fold blankets. There was no rush to leave. The air seemed lighter, as if something had shifted and everyone felt it but no one wanted to name it.

The caregiver stood slowly. The patient stayed seated on the grass, looking out toward the platform, their expression relaxed, peaceful. When the caregiver offered a hand, the patient took it and rose without hesitation. They stood easily, their posture straight, their presence steady.

A few people nearby watched with quiet smiles. One person nodded, quietly moved by what they'd seen. No one else approached. It felt like everyone understood that the space around the caregiver and patient was meant to stay open and undisturbed.

The caregiver bent to gather their things. The blanket felt different in their hands—lighter somehow. They picked up the water bottle and then reached for the wheelchair. Folding it felt strange, out of place, and for a moment they paused, unsure where to place it or what to do next. But habit guided their hands, and they collapsed the frame and stood it upright beside them.

The patient waited quietly, watching the caregiver with an expression that held neither urgency nor confusion, just presence and light.

Together they started across the field. The ground was soft underfoot. A few people stepped aside to give them room, offering small nods or quiet acknowledgments as they passed. No one asked questions.

The caregiver carried the folded wheelchair in one hand and kept the other lightly around the patient's back, not because they were unsteady now, but because it felt natural—an old gesture becoming something new.

When they reached the edge of the field, the patient slipped their hand into the caregiver's, almost casually. The caregiver glanced over, unsure who reached first, but it didn't matter. They walked the rest of the way to the car that way, hand in hand, the afternoon sun warm on their shoulders, the day closing itself gently around them.

Love and grace weren't spoken, but they were there—quiet, real, and unmistakable.
